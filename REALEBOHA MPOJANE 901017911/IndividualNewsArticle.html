A severe miscarriage of justice: Epstein survivors and families speak out in an exclusive interview

By Realeboha Mpojane| March 2026

Before Epstein files vote, survivors push for justice outside the Capitol.

In an emotional and deeply revealing exclusive interview, survivors of the late financier Jeffrey Epstein and their families have condemned what they describe as “a severe miscarriage of justice,” expressing frustration, grief and a lingering sense that true accountability has never been achieved.

More than five years after Epstein’s death in a New York jail cell in 2019, questions continue to surround not only the circumstances of his demise but also the broader system that allowed his abuse to persist for decades. For many survivors, his death marked not closure, but the loss of a critical opportunity for justice.

“Justice was taken from us,” said one survivor, who spoke on condition of anonymity. “We never got our day in court. We never got to look him in the eye and tell him what he did.”

Epstein, who was facing federal charges of sex trafficking involving minors, died while in custody at the Metropolitan Correctional Center. His death was officially ruled a suicide, but the failure of prison staff to properly monitor him sparked widespread outrage and fueled ongoing conspiracy theories. For survivors, however, the focus remains less on speculation and more on systemic failure.

Families of victims echoed these sentiments, pointing to what they believe were years of institutional negligence and missed opportunities to stop Epstein earlier. His controversial 2008 plea deal in Florida which allowed him to serve minimal jail time despite serious allegations, has been widely criticized as emblematic of a justice system that favors the powerful.

“It sent a message that people with money and connections can escape consequences,” said a relative of one survivor. “That’s the real injustice.”

Many survivors also emphasized that Epstein did not act alone. Attention has increasingly turned to his network of associates, including British socialite Ghislaine Maxwell, who was convicted in 2021 on charges related to trafficking minors. While Maxwell’s conviction provided some measure of accountability, survivors argue that others who may have enabled or participated in the abuse have yet to face scrutiny.

“There are still people out there who were part of this,” another survivor stated. “Why haven’t they been investigated? Why haven’t they been charged?”

Legal experts note that prosecuting such cases is complex, often involving international jurisdictions, powerful individuals, and decades-old evidence. Nonetheless, survivors insist that these challenges should not excuse inaction.

Beyond legal accountability, the interview also highlighted the ongoing emotional toll on victims. Many continue to struggle with trauma, stigma, and the long-term effects of abuse. Advocacy groups have called for increased support services and reforms to ensure victims are better protected in the future.

The case has also sparked broader discussions about systemic inequality within the justice system. Critics argue that Epstein’s wealth and influence allowed him to evade meaningful consequences for years, raising uncomfortable questions about fairness and accountability.

As one survivor poignantly concluded, “This isn’t just about Epstein. It’s about a system that failed us and could fail others if nothing changes.”

For survivors and their families, the fight is far from over. They continue to demand transparency, accountability, and meaningful reform—determined to ensure that their voices are heard, even in the absence of the justice they were once promised.
